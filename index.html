Words That Shaped Computing

Six voices. Six ideas. All still echoing.

The best way to predict the future is to invent it.

Alan Kay Computer Scientist & Pioneer of OOP

Alan Kay is the architect of object-oriented programming and a co-creator of the Smalltalk language at Xerox PARC in the 1970s. His vision of the Dynabook — a personal, portable computer for children — predated the laptop by decades. Kay later worked at Apple and Disney, and remains one of computing's most prophetic thinkers. He received the Turing Award in 2003.

Move fast and break things. Unless you are breaking stuff, you are not moving fast enough.

Mark Zuckerberg Co-founder & CEO, Meta

Mark Zuckerberg launched Facebook from his Harvard dorm room in 2004 at age 19, and grew it into one of the most influential platforms in history. His early engineering philosophy of radical speed over caution defined a generation of startup culture. He later pivoted the company's identity to Meta, betting billions on augmented and virtual reality as the next computing platform.

Software is eating the world.

Marc Andreessen Co-founder, Andreessen Horowitz

Marc Andreessen co-created Mosaic — the first widely used web browser — at age 22, and later co-founded Netscape, which ignited the consumer internet era. He went on to co-found the venture capital firm Andreessen Horowitz (a16z), which has backed companies like Airbnb, GitHub, and Coinbase. His 2011 Wall Street Journal essay coining "software is eating the world" remains a defining manifesto of the tech age.

Stay hungry. Stay foolish.

Steve Jobs Co-founder, Apple

Steve Jobs co-founded Apple in a garage in 1976 and went on to reimagine personal computing, animated film, music distribution, and mobile phones — four separate industries, in one lifetime. Famously ousted from Apple in 1985 and returned in 1997 to save a near-bankrupt company, he gave the world the iMac, iPod, iPhone, and iPad. He borrowed the phrase from the final issue of the Whole Earth Catalog in his 2005 Stanford commencement address.

We don't get a chance to do that many things, and every one should be really excellent. Because this is our life.

Steve Jobs Co-founder, Apple

Jobs was obsessive about craft and focus to a degree that shaped Apple's entire culture. He was known for eliminating entire product lines, demanding simplicity in design, and insisting on perfection at every layer of a product — including the circuit boards no user would ever see. This belief — that a short life demands excellence, not volume — underpinned every product Apple shipped under his leadership.

Programs must be written for people to read, and only incidentally for machines to execute.

Harold Abelson Co-author, SICP & MIT Professor

Harold Abelson is a professor of electrical engineering and computer science at MIT and a co-author of Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs (SICP) — widely regarded as one of the most important computer science textbooks ever written. A founding director of both Creative Commons and the Free Software Foundation, Abelson has spent decades arguing that programming is fundamentally a humanistic discipline, not merely a technical one.
